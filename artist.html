Meet the Architect’s Artist. Wasel has partnered with some of the biggest brands and largest real estate development companies in the world to create collaborative art pieces for public works and private clients, including Nissan, CapitaLand, Formula 1, and the Burj Khalifa, world’s tallest building. Now, he’s in San Francisco and his works are represented by the Ærena Gallery at Ma(i)sonry Napa Valley, California.

Wasel Choi and His Current Work

By Land Weismehl

This is my translation of the vibrant work of a young and talented young artist friend, Wasel Choi, whom I’ve known for a relatively short time. I’ve given myself the challenge of translating into words the non-verbal expressions he makes and of describing how his work has affected me.

Wasel spent his childhood in Al Ain City, Emirate of Abu Dhabi, UAE, has since travelled significantly throughout many parts of the world (notably on trains which are among his favorite means of transport), and now fully accepts the U.S.A., and specifically, San Francisco, as his home (it’s my long-time home city as well, creating for us a shared appreciation). That’s significant for me, not in any political sense, but in recognizing the freeing effect this city, its people, and its changing character seem to continue affecting and inspiring him.

The “here and now” aspect of his work seems to me the most significant part of how he sees and represents life. “Everything changes in space and time,” he’s said, and when the moment occurs, he “merely” records it, in sketches with his Japanese pen (his longtime favorite sketching tool); in paint with a variety of brushes and vibrant colors that remind me, when watching him in action, of the sure steady hand of a practiced calligrapher; in video when he sets up a viewing point for his 30-second “shots”; and in his sometimes succinct and often playful verbal observations.

His sketches might be called static but, in effect, are full of movement. I looked through one of his sketchbooks quite recently, saw sometimes quite minimal but meaningful observations (and even saw “something” on one of the blank pages in the series) and savored them as one might do of a fine wine or chocolate or view of an effortlessly soaring eagle. His “flat” paintings create multi-dimensions and excitement without any seeming effort or special effects. His short, timed, one-position videos might be recording movement but, in effect, are creating views that are both expansive and focused.

In his sketches and paintings and videos, I could say that his lines are graceful or bold or sensitive or daring or restful or soaring. This is how I might translate his work in text, but the words are mere shadows of what those works actually show. Translation here is the equivalent of trying to verbally describe the iridescent color of a mature blue morpho butterfly (whose colors are not from pigments at all but from a play of Nature’s light on its body scales), a mere hint and perhaps a direction to the real thing.

Life happens and Wasel has the presence to sensitively and powerfully see and record it. We could all learn from that attitude and skill.

January 12, 2015
